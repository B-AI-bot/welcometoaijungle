1. Why Build Your Own AI Infrastructure?

A personal AI infrastructure is a self-hosted system of models, tools, and automations that acts as your persistent AI assistant â€” not a chatbot you visit, but an agent that lives on your server and works for you around the clock.

You might ask: why not just use ChatGPT or Claude chat? We did, for months. Here's what broke:

  • Context dies. Every new conversation starts from zero. Our agent remembers yesterday, last week, and our entire project history.
  • No tool access. Chat interfaces can't check your email, post a tweet, search your notes, or review your calendar. Ours can.
  • No automation. You have to open the app and ask. Our agent runs morning briefs at 8 AM, evening reviews at 10 PM, and security checks every 6 hours â€” without being asked.
  • Vendor lock-in. We route between Claude and Gemini based on the task. If a model goes down or a pricing tier changes, we swap â€” no migration pain.

The goal was simple: build an AI assistant that knows our work, has access to our tools, runs 24/7, and costs less than a junior hire's coffee budget.

Memory System

Memory is what separates an agent from a chatbot. Our system has three layers:

  1. Daily Logs (memory/YYYY-MM-DD.md) â€” Raw event log. Every interaction, decision, and outcome gets recorded. The agent reads today's and yesterday's log on boot.
  2. Long-term Memory (MEMORY.md) â€” Curated, distilled knowledge. The agent periodically reviews daily logs and promotes patterns, preferences, and important facts here.
  3. Semantic Search via QMD â€” When the agent needs to recall something beyond the recent window, it runs hybrid search (BM25 + vector + reranking) across our entire Obsidian vault. This is the closest thing to actual recall.

The vault itself follows the PARA structure (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives) inside Obsidian. Everything syncs across devices via Syncthing.

6. What Doesn't Work Yet

Honesty section. Skip this at your peril if you're planning your own setup:

  • Gmail send is read-only. The agent can read and summarize email but can't send on our behalf yet. OAuth scoping and security concerns â€” we want to get this right, not fast.
  • Video pipeline is early. Remotion works, ElevenLabs works, but the end-to-end flow (script â†’ voice â†’ video â†’ upload) still needs manual handoff steps.
  • Memory needs an upgrade. Daily logs accumulate. Semantic search helps, but there's no good automated process for pruning, summarizing, and compacting old memories. It's the next big project.
  • Multi-agent coordination is primitive. Sub-agents work great for independent tasks. But when two sub-agents need to collaborate or share state, it gets messy fast.
  • Voice interaction is one-way. The agent can generate speech (ElevenLabs) but real-time voice conversation isn't there yet.

7. Lessons Learned

Seven months of running this system, distilled:

  1. Start with one channel and one model. We began with just Telegram + Claude. Every other integration came later, one at a time. If you try to build the full stack on day one, you'll build nothing.
  2. Memory is everything. The difference between "useful" and "transformative" is whether your agent remembers context. Even a simple daily log file makes a massive difference. Invest here early.
  3. Security isn't optional. Your agent has access to your email, calendar, social accounts, and files. Treat your VPS like a production server: SSH keys only, firewall everything, audit regularly. We run Fail2ban and restrict Syncthing to our Tailscale subnet.
  4. Sub-agents change everything. The moment your agent can spawn helpers for parallel work, the throughput goes through the roof. Research while writing while reviewing â€” simultaneously.
  5. Boring infrastructure wins. No containers, no orchestrators, no cloud-native buzzwords. A single VPS, systemd, and cron. When something breaks at 2 AM, you want to SSH in and read a log file, not debug a service mesh.
  6. Design for model-agnosticism. Models improve fast. Prices drop. New players emerge. If your stack is welded to one provider, you'll regret it within 6 months. Our Clawdbot gateway makes model-swapping a config change.
  7. The agent should write things down. Mental notes don't survive restarts. If the agent learns something â€” a preference, a project status, a decision â€” it writes it to a file. Disk is cheap. Amnesia is expensive.
